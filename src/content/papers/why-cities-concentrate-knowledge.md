## The short version

Cities do not matter only because they reduce shipping costs or make meetings convenient. They matter because thick environments improve matching, speed up feedback, and create repeated interactions that are hard to substitute away.

## A useful way to read the literature

I find it helpful to separate three claims that are often bundled together:

1. dense places improve productivity
2. dense places improve learning
3. dense places help ideas recombine across occupations and firms

Papers often emphasize one of these while borrowing language from the others. Keeping the distinction clear makes it easier to understand what a given empirical design can actually identify.

## What to watch for

When reading papers in this area, I usually ask:

- is the mechanism about matching, learning, or selection?
- what is the true unit of interaction?
- how local is "local" in the data?
- does the empirical strategy capture temporary shocks or persistent structure?

That checklist prevents many elegant results from sounding more general than they really are.
