## Question

Much of the empirical literature on innovation treats ideas as if they travel frictionlessly once they exist. My working premise is that this is too convenient. Knowledge may be codified, but people, routines, and local institutional ties still matter.

This project asks how urban frictions alter that movement. When it becomes harder to commute, relocate, or maintain cross-city collaboration, does knowledge stay local for longer? And if it does, what kinds of workers and firms are most affected?

## Approach

The current design combines worker mobility information, geography, and indicators of inventive activity. The goal is not only to estimate an average spillover effect, but also to understand the channels through which local knowledge stays sticky.

Three channels matter most at this stage:

- relocation and commuter adjustment
- firm-side matching and coordination
- knowledge accumulation through repeated local interactions

## Why it matters

Spatial economics often focuses on equilibrium patterns. Innovation studies often focus on inventive output. This project tries to sit between them by treating mobility frictions as part of the mechanism linking place and knowledge.

## Current status

At the moment the work is still best thought of as an organizing project page rather than a finished paper. It is where empirical decisions, mechanism questions, and related notes can accumulate as the research moves forward.
